If you've gotten more than one HVAC bid in the last decade, you've probably been quoted equipment that's bigger than your house needs. This is not a coincidence. It's not malice either. It's a combination of liability thinking, estimating habits, and a complete misunderstanding of what "too big" actually costs the homeowner.

I work in energy retrofits in Portland. I sell heat pumps and I also see what happens when the wrong heat pump gets installed. I want to explain the actual mechanics here — what Manual J is, why contractors skip it, and what goes wrong on-site when they do.

### What Manual J actually is

Manual J is the ACCA protocol for residential load calculation. It accounts for your local design temperatures (Portland's heating design temp is around 23°F), envelope insulation levels, window U-values and SHGC, infiltration rate, duct location and condition, internal gains, and occupancy. The output is a peak heating load in BTU/hr and a peak cooling load in BTU/hr. That number tells you what size system you actually need.

A proper Manual J for a 1,500 sq ft Portland home might come back at 18,000 BTU/hr heating load. That's 1.5 tons. Most contractors would put in 2.5 or 3 tons "to be safe."

The phrase "to be safe" is doing a lot of work there — almost all of it wrong.

### Why contractors oversize

There are a few real reasons this happens:

**Rules of thumb are sticky.** The "500 sq ft per ton" rule has been floating around the industry forever. In a well-insulated Portland home, it's meaningless. But it's fast, it doesn't require software, and it's what the last ten guys at the shop used.

**Liability runs one direction.** If a contractor underestimates load and the system can't maintain 70°F on a design day, that's a callback. A complaint. Maybe a warranty claim. If the system is oversized and the house is a little uncomfortable, the homeowner usually doesn't know why. There's no callback for short-cycling.

**Margins on bigger equipment.** This one is uncomfortable but real. A 3-ton unit has more margin in it than a 1.5-ton unit. Not always, but the incentive structure doesn't push toward accuracy.

**Cold-climate heat pumps changed the math.** Five years ago, sizing a heat pump for Portland heating was genuinely harder — older equipment lost capacity fast at low temps. A modern Mitsubishi Hyper Heat or Bosch IDS 2.0 holds rated capacity down to around 5°F. The backup-heat logic that justified oversizing is less applicable now, and a lot of contractors haven't updated their mental model.

### What actually goes wrong when you oversize

Oversizing isn't just an efficiency problem. It causes specific, observable failures.

**Short-cycling.** An oversized system reaches setpoint fast and shuts off. Then the house cools or heats below setpoint and it kicks on again. Short cycles mean the system runs for 5–7 minutes instead of 20–30 minutes. Compressors are most efficient in the steady middle of a run cycle, not at startup. You're paying for a lot of startups.

**Poor humidity control.** Cooling systems dehumidify by running long enough that the coil gets cold and water condenses out. A short-cycling system doesn't run long enough for the coil to reach effective dehumidification temperature. Portland summers aren't Phoenix, but we have shoulder months where you're cooling and the humidity matters. An oversized system can leave a house at 74°F and 65% relative humidity while your neighbor with a right-sized system is at 74°F and 50% RH.

**Comfort complaints that are hard to diagnose.** Short-cycling creates temperature swings. The thermostat reads fine. The homeowner says it feels drafty or uneven. The contractor sends a tech who checks refrigerant charge and airflow and finds nothing wrong. The system is performing exactly as designed — it's just the wrong design.

**Accelerated wear.** Compressors don't like frequent starts. More starts per hour means more wear on the compressor, contactor, and fan motor. An oversized system may fail years earlier than a right-sized one.

### The blower door connection

Here's the thing that doesn't get said enough: heat pump sizing and air sealing are linked. If you do a Manual J on a house with an ACH50 of 12 and then someone comes in and seals it down to 4, your load just dropped — potentially by 20–30%. The heat pump that was already marginal for the unsealed house is now definitely oversized.

In the EFH program, we often do air sealing and equipment replacement in the same scope. You need to run load calculations after air sealing is complete, or at least model the anticipated improvement and size to the post-improvement load. Most single-trade contractors who just sell heat pumps don't have this conversation because they're not doing the air sealing.

### What a proper sizing process looks like

A contractor doing this right will:

1. Pull your existing utility bills and compare heating/cooling consumption to what the software predicts. If they're wildly off, the inputs need to be refined.
2. Run Manual J with actual wall, ceiling, and window specs — not defaults. Oregon homes built before 1980 often have minimal wall insulation and single-pane windows. Oregon homes built after 2010 can be very well insulated. The inputs matter.
3. Factor in duct condition if you have a ducted system. Duct leakage to outside is a significant load multiplier.
4. Discuss the post-sealing scenario if weatherization work is happening.
5. Size to the Manual J output, with a modest buffer (BPI 2101 guidance generally allows up to 25% oversizing on heating load — not "double it").

If a contractor can't tell you what Manual J says, ask them to run it or get a second opinion from someone who will. The calculation takes about an hour for a typical house. If they're not willing to spend the hour, you're getting a guess.

### The short version

More tonnage does not mean more comfortable. It usually means less comfortable, worse humidity control, and a system that wears out faster. Manual J exists for a reason. Ask for it.
